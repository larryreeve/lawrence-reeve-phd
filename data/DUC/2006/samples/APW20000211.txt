<HEADLINE> Key Events in NIreland  </HEADLINE>
   By The Associated Press
<TEXT>
<P>
	   Key developments in Northern Ireland's struggle for peace:
</P>
<P>
	   ------
</P>
<P>
	   -- Dec. 15, 1993: British and Irish governments offer IRA-linked Sinn Fein
party a place in peace negotiations if Irish Republican Army renounces violence.
</P>
<P>
	   ------
</P>
<P>
	   -- Sept. 1, 1994: IRA begins what it calls ``complete cessation of military
operations.'' British seek assurances IRA's truce will be permanent and a
commitment to eventual disarmament -- neither forthcoming.
</P>
<P>
	   -- Dec. 9: British government opens talks with Sinn Fein leader Gerry Adams,
seeks start to IRA disarmament as price for party's entry to wider talks.
</P>
<P>
	   ------
</P>
<P>
	   -- Nov. 28, 1995: British and Irish governments appoint former U.S. Sen.
George Mitchell to resolve IRA disarmament controversy.
</P>
<P>
	   ------
</P>
<P>
	   -- Jan. 23, 1996: Mitchell proposes that IRA gradually disarm during course
of future multiparty talks involving Sinn Fein.
</P>
<P>
	   -- Feb. 9: IRA ends cease-fire, kills two men with truck bomb in London.
</P>
<P>
	   -- June 10: Multiparty talks chaired by Mitchell open in Belfast. Sinn Fein
barred.
</P>
<P>
	   ------
</P>
<P>
	   -- May 16, 1997: New British Prime Minister Tony Blair promises Sinn Fein
place in talks if IRA calls new truce.
</P>
<P>
	   -- July 20: IRA renews cease-fire.
</P>
<P>
	   ------
</P>
<P>
	   -- March 25, 1998: Mitchell sets April 9 as agreement deadline.
</P>
<P>
	   -- April 10: Eight parties accept accord envisioning a Protestant-Catholic
administration, links with the Irish Republic, and gradual disarmament of
paramilitary groups.
</P>
<P>
	   -- May 22: Voters approve accord in Northern Ireland and Irish Republic.
</P>
<P>
	   -- July 1: David Trimble, Protestant leader of the Ulster Unionist Party,
elected future senior minister of government. Says Sinn Fein won't get Cabinet
posts unless IRA starts to disarm.
</P>
<P>
	   -- Sept. 10: Trimble and Adams hold first one-on-one meeting.
</P>
<P>
	   -- Oct. 16: Trimble and John Hume, moderate Catholic leader of Social
Democratic and Labor Party, win Nobel Peace Prize.
</P>
<P>
	   -- Oct. 31: De-facto deadline for appointing new Cabinet passes without
agreement.
</P>
<P>
	   ------
</P>
<P>
	   -- March 10, 1999: Second deadline for appointing Cabinet passes.
</P>
<P>
	   -- May 15: After IRA refuses to make disarmament commitment, Blair announces
``absolute deadline'' of June 30 for resolution.
</P>
<P>
	   -- July 2: Blair unveils ``way forward'' document proposing that IRA
disarmament commences days after Cabinet's formation. Trimble rejects.
</P>
<P>
	   -- July 15: Attempt to elect Cabinet fails because of Protestant boycott.
</P>
<P>
	   -- Nov. 18: Mitchell brokers plan that requires Ulster Unionists to accept
Sinn Fein in Cabinet, Sinn Fein to pledge disarmament is ``essential,'' and the
IRA to start cooperating with disarmament commission.
</P>
<P>
	   -- Nov. 27: Trimble wins 58 percent support from Ulster Unionist grassroots
to form four-party Cabinet; promises to resign if IRA hasn't begun disarming by
February.
</P>
<P>
	   ------
</P>
<P>
	   -- Jan. 31, 2000: Disarmament commission tells British and Irish governments
the IRA has made no disarmament commitments.
</P>
<P>
	   -- Feb. 3: Irish government presses IRA to move; British announce plans to
strip the Cabinet of powers as the most likely way to keep Ulster Unionists
involved.
</P>
<P>
	   -- Feb. 11: Britain resumes sole control of Northern Ireland. British and
Irish governments say new IRA proposal softens traditional stance on arms. New
disarmament commission report says it has made significant progress in talks
with the IRA.
</P>
